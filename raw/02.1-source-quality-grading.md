# Art History Source Evaluation Framework: Quality Scoring Rubric & Heuristics

### 1. Framework Foundations: The "Art-Historical Lateral Reading" Approach

In the contemporary digital landscape, art-historical research is increasingly susceptible to "superficial markers of credibility." High-resolution images of canonical works—such as the *Aphrodite of Cnidos* or Fragonard’s *The Swing*—can lend a deceptive "institutional stamp of approval" to sites that are actually commercially driven content aggregators. Traditional "vertical reading" (staying within a site to judge its quality) is no longer sufficient. Sophisticated SEO farms often present professionally designed "About Us" pages, but these are frequently self-authored narratives devoid of external verification.

This framework advocates for a shift from the CRAAP test toward the **SIFT method** (Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace claims) and the practice of **Art-Historical Lateral Reading**. We must move beyond the site’s own interface to trace the provenance of claims and verify the author’s standing within the scholarly community. Special attention must be paid to "closed loops" of information, where non-credible sites utilize internal linking—citing only their own blog posts—to create a false sense of authority.

> **Lateral Reading Definition**
> The practice of leaving a website to verify its claims and check its credibility by consulting other sources. This method identifies biases, funding sources, and institutional affiliations by seeking external perspectives and fact-checking resources rather than relying on the site's self-presentation.

### 2. The Weighted Scoring Rubric for Art History

This rubric prioritizes scholarly originality and the "Open Educational Resource" (OER) movement over commercially motivated content.

| Criterion | Weight | High-Quality Indicators | Red Flags | Score (1-5) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Institutional Authority** | 30% | Authors hold terminal degrees (PhD/MA) as per CAA standards; affiliation with accredited museums (MoMA, The Met) or universities. | Anonymous authors; "Art Marketing" contributors; lack of specialist credentials; self-authored "About Us" verification. | |
| **Depth & Scholarly Originality** | 25% | Models "Close Looking"; unscripted dialogues between specialists; essays exceed 1,000 words (vs. 550-word textbook summaries). | Shallow "Top 10" lists; generic metadata; content derived from "Trends" or "Buyer's Guides." | |
| **Commercial Bias & Purpose** | 20% | Clear educational mission; funded by non-profit foundations (Kress, Mellon); ad-free or minimal/relevant scholarly ads. | Affiliate marketing markers (Tudeeo, Displate, Saatchi); focus on "Monetization" or "Earn extra income"; "Add to Cart" buttons. | |
| **Accuracy & Citation Quality** | 15% | Traces provenance of claims with robust external bibliographies; links to primary museum records or peer-reviewed journals. | "Internal linking" only (closed information loops); lack of external references; no <footer> with bibliographic data. | |
| **Usability & Ad Density** | 10% | 50+ high-resolution photos/diagrams per object; focus-oriented layout; high image-to-text contextual relevance. | Ad-saturated layouts that disrupt study; single cropped thumbnails; "Shopping Cart 0" navigation prominently displayed. | |

### 3. Tier Definitions: From High-Trust to Avoid

*   **Tier 1: Scholarly/Institutional (Gold):** The elite standard for OER. Backed by specialists with terminal degrees, these sites prioritize visual analysis over monetization. Example: **Smarthistory**, which utilizes 200+ specialist contributors to create unscripted, historically grounded dialogues.
*   **Tier 2: Useful-but-Limited (Silver):** High-quality journalism or professional blogs (e.g., *caa.reviews*). While expert-led, they may feature ad-supported models or broader, less specialized visual evidence.
*   **Tier 3: Reference/Data-Only (Bronze):** Auction houses (Sotheby's, Christie's) or aggregate databases. Essential for raw facts—dimensions, sales history, and provenance—but lack the critical "historiographical rigor" required for deep learning.
*   **Tier 4: Commercially Biased:** Sites existing primarily to drive conversions (e.g., **Tudeeo**, **Society6**). These "artist-first" platforms use art history as a "lure" for affiliate commissions (e.g., metal prints, posters).
*   **Tier 5: Low-Quality/Avoidable:** Shallow SEO farms and aggregators. Characterized by high "internal linking," lack of specialist oversight, and a focus on "monetization" over education.

### 4. Detectable Signals: Machine-Observable Heuristics

Digital humanities scrapers or researchers can automate quality assessment by looking for these specific technical cues:

*   **Commerce Strings:** Scan for `Add to Cart`, `Buy Print`, `Cookie Duration`, `12% Commission`, `High-Ticket`, or `Shopping Cart 0`.
*   **Domain/TLD Nuance:** Treat **.edu** and **.gov** as high-trust, but note that **.org** is no longer a definitive marker of credibility, as it is frequently used by biased or partisan entities.
*   **Citation Tags:** Presence of `<footer>`, `<section id="references">`, or `Recommended Citation` blocks (e.g., JSU Digital Commons models).
*   **Visual Complexity:** High trust signals include a high ratio of `<img>` tags per article (Smarthistory models 50+ per object) versus single-image "thumbnails" typical of affiliate blogs.
*   **Language Cues:**
    *   **Low Trust:** "Monetization," "Artist-first," "Earn extra income," "Buyer’s Guide," "Trending now."
    *   **High Trust:** "Visual analysis," "Historiography," "Male Gaze," "Provenance," "Terminal degree," "Peer-reviewed."

### 5. Shortlist: Consistently High-Trust Art History Domains

*   **Smarthistory.org:** The global leader in OER. Note its 35M annual views (2016) significantly outpace the Met's Heilbrunn Timeline (10.4M), demonstrating the massive impact of open-access, specialist-authored content.
*   **The Met (Heilbrunn Timeline):** Institutional gold standard for curated essays and high-resolution primary source access.
*   **College Art Association (CAA):** The definitive source for professional standards and peer-reviewed guidelines.
*   **JSU Digital Commons:** A prime example of university-backed repositories hosting rigorous feminist scholarship like the Weaver thesis.
