# Research prompt — Human-in-the-loop curation UX for selecting artworks to study

I'm building a tool with a **human-in-the-loop curation step**: after an automated process
gathers a gallery of candidate artwork images, the artist reviews them and selects a
**shortlist** for deep visual analysis and master study. I need research and best practices
on **presenting candidates** and **supporting good selection decisions** without removing
human judgment.

Cover:

- **Visual triage / culling workflows** — how photographers and editors cull large image
  pools (contact sheets, ratings/flags/picks, compare/survey views; Lightroom, Photo
  Mechanic, capture-one patterns). What transfers to curating artworks for study.
- **Choice architecture** — avoiding choice overload; ideal candidate-set size; progressive
  narrowing (cull-then-rank); defaults and pre-filtering that inform without deciding.
- **What to show per candidate** — which metadata aids a study decision (source, resolution,
  trust grade, period, medium, why-it-matters) and how to lay it out for fast comparison.
- **Selection heuristics for study** (not for taste alone) — balancing variety of
  periods/techniques, personal interest, and *learnability* at the user's level; when to
  pick similar works for focused repetition vs. diverse works for breadth.
- **Productive friction** — keeping the human genuinely in control; where light automation
  helps and where it would short-circuit learning.

Conclude with: (a) a **recommended curation workflow** (steps and views); (b) **what to
display per candidate** and the overall gallery layout; and (c) concrete **selection-support
artifacts** the tool could generate — e.g., a selection worksheet, comparison prompts, or a
shortlist rationale template — and how the shortlist hands off to deep analysis. Cite
reputable HCI, curation, and visual-workflow sources.
