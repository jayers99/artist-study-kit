# Technical Taxonomy and Methodology for Master Study Analysis

## 1. The Layered Taxonomy of Formal Analysis

The practitioner must isolate the abstract architecture of a work before attempting to replicate its physical illusion. This methodology follows a rigorous "General to Specific" progression, evolving from the capture of abstract energy to the execution of precise physical form.

*   **Level 1: Macro-Structure (Composition & Value Masses)**
    *   **The Structural Skeleton (Dow’s Five Principles):**
        *   *Opposition:* Two lines meeting at right angles to create stability (e.g., the meeting of a floor and a wall, or a corner of a room).
        *   *Transition:* The use of curves or softening elements to bridge opposing lines.
        *   *Subordination:* Organizing elements so they relate to a single, dominating feature.
        *   *Repetition:* The rhythmic use of similar elements.
        *   *Symmetry:* Balanced distribution across an axis.
    *   **Notan Structure:** This is not a value sketch, but the "Dark-Light" balance and distribution of dark forms against light forms (the Yin-Yang principle).
        *   *2-Value Notan:* Reducing the image to pure black and white shapes to assess rhythmic harmony and positive/negative space.
        *   *3-Value Notan:* Introducing a mid-tone to bridge masses without losing the abstract power of the design.

*   **Level 2: Chromatic Strategy (Color & Temperature)**
    *   **Palette Archeology:**
        *   *The Zorn Palette:* A limited system of Ivory Black, Cadmium Red, Yellow Ochre, and Titanium White. The practitioner must recognize that Ivory Black serves as the substitute for blue, creating a unique, muted harmony.
        *   *Split Primary System:* Utilizing warm and cool variations of each primary to mix a wider gamut (e.g., Alizarin Crimson for cool reds vs. Cadmium Red for warm reds).
    *   **Temperature & Chroma:** Identification of the interplay between warm lights and cool shadows. Use high chroma (saturation) to define light masses, while muting shadows to establish depth.

*   **Level 3: Form Construction (Volume & Value)**
    *   **Modified Grisaille (Ramos Technique):**
        *   *First Flesh Pass:* A medium-value mixture (e.g., Cadmium Red Light and Permanent Green Light). **Crucially, no white is added at this stage** to maintain depth.
        *   *The Opaque Pass ("Putting on Skin"):* Adding Titanium White to the flesh mixture to build bone structure and create volume in light-struck areas.
    *   **Value Planning & Substrate Integrity:** Mapping the range from the darkest darks to the lightest highlights. The practitioner should use a sponge brush or cotton ball to obscure the white of the substrate early, preventing "pinholes."
    *   **Substrate-induced Discoloration:** To preserve the work, one must understand that inherent natural oils can migrate through paint capillaries; sealing with GAC 100 or gesso is a formal necessity.

*   **Level 4: Surface Handling (Mark-Making & Texture)**
    *   **Optical Modification (Kinnear’s Logic):**
        *   *Glazing:* Applying an unbroken, translucent film of paint. This reduces contrast and unifies color.
        *   *Scumbling:* Applying a broken, "sticky" paint film over a dry underlayer. This increases optical complexity and textural richness.
    *   **The General to Specific Progression:** Moving from large flat decorating brushes for blocking masses to smaller, specific tools for final "Invenzioni" (inventive details).

## 3. The Repeatable Analysis Workflow (LLM Instruction Set)

To execute a master study analysis, command the LLM using the following stage-gate prompts:

1.  **Stage 1: Structural Skeleton:** "Analyze the uploaded image and identify its primary landmarks. Describe the composition using Dow's Five Principles (Opposition, Transition, Subordination, Repetition, Symmetry), specifically identifying where lines meet at right angles or where curves bridge those oppositions."
2.  **Stage 2: Notan Mapping:** "Reduce this image to an abstract 2-value or 3-value pattern. Evaluate its 'Yin-Yang' balance—how the distribution of dark and light forms creates a rhythmic harmony that functions independently of the subject matter."
3.  **Stage 3: Palette Archeology:** "Identify the palette. If limited, determine if it follows the Zorn system, noting how black may be substituting for blue. Describe the temperature strategy: are the lights high-chroma/warm and the shadows muted/cool, or vice versa?"
4.  **Stage 4: Technical Layering:** "Hypothesize the layering sequence. Identify the initial transparent wash (e.g., Burnt Sienna), the first flesh pass (Cad Red Light/Perm Green Light without white), and the subsequent 'putting on skin' opaque layers where Titanium White was introduced for volume."
5.  **Stage 5: Traps & Misconceptions:** "Critically examine the work for 'milkiness' or 'chalkiness.' Pinpoint where the artist may have prematurely added white to transparent passages, thus losing depth, and identify where scumbling was used to increase optical complexity rather than simply blending."

## 4. Targeted Master Study Exercises

*   **The "Egg" Drill (Witheridge):** Master the Zorn palette by painting a white egg under a single light source. Focus on value shifts and maintaining the transparency of the shadow—adding white to the tea "loses the transparency for good."
*   **Value Thumbnails (Dow/Berry):** Create small-scale 2-value Notan sketches. Avoid "gray" or "blurry" sketches; focus purely on the balance of dark forms against the light ground to internalize the abstract architecture.
*   **Limited Palette Challenge (Zorn):** Re-create a passage using only Ivory Black, Cadmium Red, Yellow Ochre, and Titanium White. Experiment with using Black + White to create "blue" grays.
*   **The Scumbling Study (Kinnear):** Practice applying a broken paint film of thick, sticky paint (mixed with chalk if necessary) over a dry, contrasting underpainting. Observe how this increases optical complexity compared to a smooth glaze.
*   **Modified Grisaille Pass (Ramos):** Execute a head study in two specific passes: Step 1, block in features with a Cad Red/Perm Green mix (no white); Step 2, "put on skin" by adding Titanium White only to the areas requiring volume and light.
