# Research prompt — A productive-friction framework for AI-assisted learning

I'm building an AI-assisted study tool and want it to **strengthen the learner's memory and understanding by adding the right amount of friction** — not spoon-feeding answers. Research the learning science and the prior art so I can design this well.

Cover the cognitive-science foundations:
- **Desirable difficulties** (Bjork): why effortful retrieval and slower acquisition improve long-term retention.
- **Retrieval practice / the testing effect**, the **generation effect**, and **spacing** vs. massed practice.
- **Interleaving** vs. blocking, and the difference between **desirable** and **undesirable** difficulty (when friction helps vs. just frustrates).
- **Cognitive load theory** and the **expertise-reversal effect** — how optimal scaffolding changes as the learner improves.
- The **zone of proximal development** and adaptive challenge.

Cover the applied / AI prior art:
- **Khan Academy's Khanmigo** and its design choice to guide Socratically rather than give answers — what worked, what they learned, reported outcomes.
- **Intelligent tutoring systems**, Socratic questioning, **worked-example fading**, and hint-laddering.
- **Human-in-the-loop** designs that require the learner to commit an answer or attempt before help is revealed.
- Known **failure modes** of AI tutors (answer-leakage, over-helping, gaming).

Conclude with: (a) a concise **framework / set of principles** for calibrating friction for a motivated adult self-learner; (b) **concrete interaction patterns** an AI study assistant should use (e.g. predict-then-reveal, retrieval prompts, spaced check-ins, Socratic critique); and (c) **anti-patterns** to avoid. Make it actionable for an art-study context. Cite research and reputable sources.
