# Research prompt — Designing effective cheat sheets and scaffolded study aids

I'm building a study tool that will offer **cheat sheets, checklists, reference cards, and worked examples** to support an artist's learning. I want these aids to genuinely build skill and understanding — not become crutches that short-circuit learning. Research how to design them well.

Cover the principles and prior art:
- **Cognitive load theory** applied to reference materials: the **worked-example effect**, **split-attention** and **redundancy** effects, and **completion problems**.
- **Scaffolding and fading** — providing support early and systematically removing it as competence grows; how to design aids that are meant to be outgrown.
- The distinction between a **job aid / performance support** (meant to be used forever) and a **learning aid** (meant to be internalized then dropped) — and which is appropriate when.
- What makes a **cheat sheet effective**: chunking, visual hierarchy, retrieval cues, "minimal but sufficient," and the value of **learner-generated** summaries vs. provided ones.
- Checklists as cognitive tools (e.g. the value of structured checklists for complex, error-prone tasks).
- When an aid **helps** vs. when it **harms** retention (the crutch problem, fluency illusions).

Conclude with: (a) **design principles** for study aids that support without spoon-feeding; (b) a set of **templates/formats** suited to an art-study context (e.g. a style cheat sheet, a value/composition checklist, a technique reference card, a "what to notice first" prompt); and (c) guidance on **when to provide an aid vs. ask the learner to build their own**. Make it concrete and actionable. Cite reputable instructional-design and cognitive-science sources.
