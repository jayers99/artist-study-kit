# Research prompt — Master-study pedagogy and prior art in art education

I'm building a tool that guides a self-directed artist through an effective **master study** of a historical artist. I need to understand the **prior art in art education**: how copying and studying masters is used as a teaching method, so I can design a learning process grounded in established pedagogy rather than inventing one.

Research how master studies / master copies are taught, drawing on:
- **The Art Institute of Chicago** and its art-school traditions, plus other leading art schools and academies.
- The **atelier method** and the **academic tradition** (cast drawing, the **Bargue** drawing course, sight-size).
- **Museum copyist programs** (e.g. the Louvre copyist tradition) and why institutions encourage copying.
- Historical apprenticeship models (workshop/bottega copying).

For each tradition, surface:
- The **learning objectives** — what copying a master is meant to teach (observation, value, drawing, color, technique, taste).
- The **sequencing / curriculum** — how studies are staged from simple to complex, and prerequisites.
- **Methods of study** — partial vs. full copies, drawing before painting, limited-scope studies (a single passage, the value structure, the palette).
- **Critique and feedback** practices that make a study educational rather than mechanical.
- Common **pitfalls** the tradition warns against (slavish copying without understanding, copying photos vs. originals, scale issues).

Conclude with: (a) a synthesis of **principles for an effective master study** that recur across traditions; (b) a recommended **staged workflow** for a motivated adult self-learner; and (c) how an AI assistant could support each stage without replacing the learner's own looking and doing. Favor art-school, atelier, museum, and academic sources; cite them.
