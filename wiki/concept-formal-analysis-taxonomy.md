# Formal-Analysis Taxonomy

A "general-to-specific" framework for reading a work's abstract architecture before its
physical surface. Source: [[04.1-style-analysis-frameworks]]. Used by
[[stage-style-definition]] (recognizing the grammar up front) and [[stage-visual-analysis]]
(deep-reading a curated work).

## The four levels

1. **Macro-structure — composition & value masses.** *Dow's five principles* (opposition,
   transition, subordination, repetition, symmetry) give the structural skeleton.
   *Notan* is the dark/light balance (Yin-Yang): reduce to 2-value or 3-value patterns to
   judge rhythmic harmony independent of subject.
2. **Chromatic strategy — color & temperature.** *Palette archeology*: identify limited
   systems like the **Zorn palette** (ivory black, cad red, yellow ochre, white — black
   substitutes for blue) or split-primary. Read warm-light/cool-shadow logic and chroma.
3. **Form construction — volume & value.** Modeling sequence: transparent first pass (no
   white) → opaque "skin" pass (white added for volume). Value planning from darkest dark
   to lightest highlight.
4. **Surface handling — mark-making & texture.** *Glazing* (translucent, unifies, lowers
   contrast) vs *scumbling* (broken, sticky film, raises optical complexity). Brush
   progression: large/general → small/specific.

## Why it matters for study

Isolating the abstract design (Notan, Dow) before the illusion is what makes a master copy
*instructive* rather than slavish — it trains the eye on decisions, not just appearances.
The taxonomy doubles as a vocabulary: each level supplies precise terms an analysis or
study note can use ("subordination", "chroma", "scumbling") instead of vague description.

## Operationalization

[[04.1-style-analysis-frameworks]] turns the taxonomy into a 5-stage LLM instruction set
(structural skeleton → Notan mapping → palette archeology → technical layering → traps),
plus drills (the egg/Zorn drill, value thumbnails, scumbling study) and a reusable
per-work template. These feed the artifacts in [[stage-visual-analysis]].
